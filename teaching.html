Hitotsubashi University

    Labor Economics

    (Undergraduate, Winter 2016)

    The objective of the course is to provide students with basic economic tools needed to understand and analyze how labor markets work and how various government policies may affect the outcomes of the market. We discuss theory, policy applications, causal inference, and relevant empirical research throughout the course.

University of Western Ontario

  • Intermediate Microeconomic Theory II

    • (Econ 2151B-001, Undergraduate, Summer 2015)

    This course is the second in the microeconomic theory sequence and will focus on the behaviour of individuals participating in markets that are not necessarily competitive. Building on the tools developed in Micro I, we first consider cases where firms have market power, or the ability to manipulate market outcomes through oneâ€™s own actions. We will study various imperfectly competitive market structures and show how competition affects consumers, producers and the economy as a whole. This course will introduce new analytical tools in order to study how individuals can act strategically to manipulate market outcomes, which will allow us to have new insights into the effects of economic policy in strategic environments. Finally, this course will discuss competitive markets that are unlikely to achieve efficient resource allocations, by introducing the concept of externalities and public goods.
